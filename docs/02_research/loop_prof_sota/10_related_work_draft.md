This chapter reviews the research and operational landscape against which the thesis is positioned. The structure follows the logic of the central research question — *what added value does multiband satellite imagery provide over RGB for discriminating the materials that determine the hazard of an illegal waste site?* Section 1 establishes the problem and reframes it from detection to risk classification, anchoring the technical objective in the regulatory and epidemiological notion of risk. Section 2 surveys the state of RGB-based waste detection from very-high-resolution (VHR) imagery, which is shown to be a largely mature but deliberately material-blind capability. Section 3 develops the physical and empirical case for multispectral material discrimination, covering the spectral basis of material identity and the two most informative precedents — asbestos and plastics — together with their honest limitations. Section 4 examines the sensor and dataset landscape, making explicit the spatial-versus-spectral trade-off and the structural data gap that conditions any experimental design. Section 5 turns to the methodological machinery — adapting pretrained backbones to N bands, spectral foundation models, native-resolution analysis, and generalization. The chapter closes with a synthesis of the gaps in the literature and a hedged statement of how this work is positioned against them.

Throughout, Earth Observation (EO) denotes satellite or airborne remote sensing of the Earth's surface; multispectral (MS) imagery comprises a small number of broad spectral bands, in contrast to hyperspectral imagery (HSI), which samples hundreds of contiguous narrow bands; VNIR and SWIR denote the visible-and-near-infrared and shortwave-infrared portions of the spectrum, respectively; and GSD (ground sampling distance) is the on-ground size of one image pixel. These terms are used consistently below.

## 1. Illegal Waste as Environmental Crime: From Detection to Risk Classification

The illegal disposal and abandonment of waste is a persistent and economically significant form of environmental crime across the European Union, with documented harm to soil, groundwater, air quality, and public health. In the Italian context the problem is acute in regions with dense industrial activity and a history of organised illegal trafficking, where unauthorised dumpsites range from scattered fly-tipping to large clandestine deposits of construction, demolition, and industrial residues. Regional environmental protection agencies (the *Agenzie Regionali per la Protezione dell'Ambiente*, ARPA) are tasked with locating, characterising, and prioritising these sites for inspection and remediation, but operate under tight resource constraints that make exhaustive ground survey impractical. This asymmetry — many candidate sites, finite enforcement capacity — motivates the use of EO as a triage instrument, and it reframes the technical objective: the operationally useful output is not merely *where* waste is, but *which* sites warrant intervention first.

The regulatory backbone for this prioritisation is the European Waste Catalogue and its annexed List of Waste, which assigns each waste stream a code and flags hazardous entries with an asterisk; asbestos-containing materials, for instance, fall under code 17 06 05*. Hazard classification at the material level is therefore not a research convenience but a legal category that directly conditions the enforcement response. Italian instruments add an operational layer: regional asbestos mapping programmes (the *Piani Regionali Amianto*, PRAL) and degradation indices such as ARPA's *Indice di Degrado* translate material condition into intervention priority. These frameworks share a common logic that the risk literature makes explicit. Following the formulation popularised in environmental epidemiology — risk as a function of hazard, exposure, and magnitude (Fazzo et al., 2020) — the priority assigned to a candidate site depends jointly on *what* the material is (its intrinsic hazard), *who and what* it can reach (exposure pathways such as proximity to settlements or watercourses), and *how much* of it is present. Two of these three factors are material-dependent, which is precisely where purely geometric, RGB-based detection reaches its ceiling: a model can outline a deposit and estimate its extent, but cannot, from colour and texture alone, reliably tell hazardous from inert material.

This thesis is situated within the PERIVALLON project (Horizon Europe, Grant 101073952), which targets the protection of the environment from illegal activities through advanced sensing and analytics. The contribution pursued here is the shift from *detection* to *risk classification* of waste sites from VHR satellite imagery, using multiband sensors — WorldView-3 (eight VNIR bands at ~1.24 m, eight SWIR bands at ~3.7 m, and a ~0.31 m panchromatic channel) and Pléiades Neo (six VNIR bands at ~1.2 m, ~0.3 m panchromatic) — to recover the material information that RGB cannot. As a limitation, we note that risk in the Fazzo sense also depends on exposure and magnitude factors that are partly exogenous to the imagery (e.g. population proximity derived from ancillary GIS layers); the present work concentrates on the *material/hazard* axis, where the added value of spectral information is most directly testable, while acknowledging that a complete operational risk score would integrate these external factors.

## 2. RGB-Based Detection from VHR Imagery

The detection of waste and dumpsites from overhead imagery has matured rapidly and can, for many settings, be considered largely solved — with the important caveat that "solved" here means *localisation from shape and context*, not material characterisation. The reference dataset for the Italian setting is AerialWaste (Torres and Fraternali, 2023), a large-scale collection of VHR aerial tiles annotated for illegal-landfill detection, with 22 fine-grained material categories and site-level labels; for confidentiality and enforcement reasons the precise geographic coordinates of positive sites are withheld. AerialWaste is explicitly RGB-only, a property that constrains every model trained on it and that the authors' own broader survey identifies as a structural limitation of the field: existing remote-sensing approaches to waste detection rely overwhelmingly on three visible channels and therefore exploit spatial cues — object shape, spatial arrangement, surrounding land use — rather than material spectra (Fraternali et al., 2024).

The current strongest result on this dataset comes from Gibellini (2025), who fine-tunes a Swin-T transformer backbone initialised with Remote-Sensing-Pretraining (RSP) weights, reporting an F1 of 92.02% for landfill classification. Crucially, the same work documents a cross-region performance drop of roughly 5.1%, exposing the generalization gap that appears when a model trained in one geographic area is applied to another — a recurring theme in EO and a direct consequence of context-dependent learning. Ensemble configurations over the AerialWaste data have been explored to improve robustness, but these inherit the same RGB-only ceiling: they sharpen the geometric decision boundary without adding any material discriminability.

Comparable performance is reported on satellite (rather than aerial) imagery. CascadeDumpNet (2024) operates on Pléiades imagery at ~0.5 m and reports 84.6% mAP for dumpsite detection, confirming that spaceborne VHR sensors are now adequate for the localisation task and narrowing the historical gap with airborne platforms. At global scale, Sun et al. (2023) demonstrate dumpsite mapping across heterogeneous geographies, establishing the feasibility of wide-area screening while also surfacing the domain-shift and label-scarcity problems that dominate large-area deployment. On the operational side, commercial providers report that Pléiades Neo-based pipelines reach detection rates on the order of 95% (Disaitek/Airbus); these figures are vendor-reported and not independently benchmarked, so they should be read as indicative of industrial maturity rather than as comparable scientific results.

Taken together, this body of work supports a clear and deliberately bounded conclusion. Detection of waste sites from VHR imagery is a well-developed capability: multiple independent efforts — aerial and spaceborne, academic and commercial — converge on high localisation accuracy by leveraging shape and spatial context. Yet the same literature is uniformly RGB-based and therefore *material-blind*: it can find a site and delineate its footprint, but cannot, from three visible bands, infer whether the deposited material is hazardous. None of the works reviewed here was designed to answer that question, so they establish a strong detection baseline while leaving the material axis essentially untested. This is the precise limitation that the risk-classification framing of this thesis aims to address, and it motivates the empirical question taken up next — whether and to what extent multiband VNIR and SWIR information adds value over RGB for material discrimination.

## 3. Multispectral Material Discrimination

### 3.1 The Spectral Basis of Material Identity

The premise motivating a move beyond RGB imagery is that the chemical identity of a material is encoded in its reflectance spectrum, and that the diagnostic features lie predominantly *outside* the visible range. Electronic transitions and vibrational absorptions tied to specific molecular bonds produce narrow, characteristic absorption features in the near-infrared and shortwave-infrared. For the materials relevant to illegal-waste characterisation, three families of features are diagnostic. Chrysotile and other serpentine-group asbestos minerals exhibit a strong Mg-OH absorption near 2.30-2.33 µm, the basis for hyperspectral asbestos mapping in the literature (Kokaly et al., 2017; Shepherd et al., 2025). Plastics and other hydrocarbon polymers show C-H overtone and combination features near 1.215 µm and 1.730 µm, which underpin the normalized difference plastic indices used for marine and terrestrial plastic detection (Aguilar et al., 2021). Carbonates — common in construction-and-demolition debris and in asbestos-cement (AC) matrices — produce a CO₃ absorption near 2.34 µm. None of these features is accessible to a three-band RGB sensor, which is precisely the limitation that the broader remote-sensing waste literature identifies as fundamental rather than incidental (Fraternali et al., 2024).

This physical argument frames the object-versus-material split that organises the remainder of the section. A growing body of VHR work treats waste as an *object-detection* or *scene-classification* problem, learning the visual gestalt of a dumpsite — texture, context, spatial arrangement — from RGB tiles (Torres and Fraternali, 2023; Sun et al., 2023). This is effective for *localisation* but is, by construction, blind to *what* a site contains. Material discrimination is a distinct problem: it asks not "is there waste here?" but "is this pile hazardous?", and the evidence above indicates that the answer is carried by spectral channels that RGB-only object detectors do not observe.

### 3.2 The Asbestos Line

Asbestos-cement roofing is the most studied material-discrimination target and the natural entry point for this thesis. The hyperspectral end of the spectrum establishes the upper bound: Shepherd et al. (2025) report an Adaptive Cosine Estimator detection accuracy of 91.4% for AC roofs using spaceborne EnMAP imagery, exploiting the full SWIR absorption complex — though at 30 m GSD, EnMAP falls outside the VHR regime this work targets. Airborne hyperspectral studies similarly link spectral indices to weathering state: Cilia et al. (2015) use MIVIS data to relate an ISDVeg-type index to roof degradation, and Martínez-Sánchez et al. (2024) derive AC-degradation indices over a controlled set of 149 roofs. The multispectral VHR results most relevant here come from WorldView-3 (WV-3). Saba et al. (2026) report a Macro-F1 of 97.6% for asbestos classification using a Fine-KNN classifier on WV-3 VNIR bands, and Bonifazi et al. (2026) report VNIR+SWIR classification of AC roofs (an F1 of 0.87 is cited in pre-publication material but remains unverified and is not relied upon here).

Two caveats temper these numbers. First, several headline comparisons — for instance, claims that hyperspectral dramatically outperforms multispectral, or specific kappa-improvement figures attributed to SWIR — are not reliably documented and should be treated as motivating rather than established. Second, the asbestos task benefits from a uniquely sharp diagnostic feature (Mg-OH at 2.30-2.33 µm); generalisation of these accuracies to heterogeneous mixed-waste piles is an open question.

### 3.3 The Plastics Line and Urban-Material Precedents

The plastics literature provides the second pillar and a useful methodological analogy. Aguilar et al. (2021) conduct a WV-3 band ablation for plastic-covered greenhouse mapping, reporting overall accuracies of 90.85% (VNIR), 96.79%, and 97.38% as SWIR bands are progressively added — a result that directly supports the band-ablation design adopted in this thesis and motivates a plastic-film index analogous to the normalized difference plastic index (NDPI). At the hyperspectral extreme, spaceborne imaging spectrometers such as EMIT have demonstrated detection of plastics and other anthropogenic materials via their C-H signatures, and a parallel body of work targets floating marine plastics (Zhou et al., 2022). Beyond asbestos and plastics, urban-material spectral libraries and classification studies establish that concrete, bitumen, metals, and vegetation are separable in VNIR-SWIR feature space, providing precedent that the *mixed* materials of a real dumpsite are, in principle, spectrally distinguishable.

### 3.4 Honest Limitations: The SWIR-8 Bottleneck and Mixed Pixels

Two limitations bound what VHR multispectral data can deliver. The first is the SWIR-8 bottleneck: WV-3 provides only eight SWIR bands at 3.7 m GSD, and the diagnostic features of asbestos (Mg-OH ~2.33 µm), concrete carbonate (~2.34 µm), and certain plastics crowd into essentially a single SWIR-8 band. A multispectral sensor therefore *coarsely samples* an absorption complex that a hyperspectral instrument resolves continuously, limiting separability among chemically similar materials and making the multispectral-versus-hyperspectral gap material-dependent rather than uniform. The second is the mixed-pixel problem: at 1.2-3.7 m GSD a dumpsite pixel is a spatial mixture of materials, soil, vegetation, and shadow, so the observed spectrum is a (typically nonlinear) blend rather than a pure endmember. Spectral unmixing is treated as out of experimental scope here; the consequence is that this work measures *material-discriminative value at the pixel/patch level*, not pure-material identification. These two constraints are why the thesis frames its contribution as a measured *added value of multiband over RGB* via controlled ablation, rather than a claim of definitive material identification.

## 4. Sensors and Data

### 4.1 The GSD-versus-Spectral-Resolution Trade-off

No single operational sensor simultaneously offers fine GSD, broad spectral coverage, and frequent revisit; sensor choice is therefore a trade-off that the literature makes explicit. At one extreme, Sentinel-2 offers free, frequent, 13-band imagery but at 10-20 m GSD — far too coarse to resolve individual dumpsites or roofs, which occupy only a handful of pixels. Spaceborne imaging spectrometers (EnMAP, PRISMA) provide hundreds of contiguous bands and full SWIR coverage but at ~30 m GSD, again outside the VHR regime needed for site-level intervention (Shepherd et al., 2025). At the VHR end, WorldView-3 (8 VNIR bands at 1.24 m, 8 SWIR at 3.7 m, panchromatic at 0.31 m), Pléiades Neo (6 VNIR at 1.2 m, pan at 0.3 m), and the SuperDove constellation (8 VNIR bands at ~3 m, no SWIR) deliver the spatial detail required, at the cost of fewer bands and — critically — limited or absent SWIR. This thesis sits deliberately in the VHR multispectral corner: WV-3 is the only platform combining sub-2 m VNIR with any SWIR coverage, making it the reference sensor for the ablation that extends from RGB (R0) through RedEdge/NIR (R1) and full VNIR (R2) to the SWIR-augmented configuration (R3).

### 4.2 The Dataset Landscape and the Structural Gap

The supervised-learning literature on remote-sensing waste rests on a small number of datasets, none of which fully serves the material-discrimination task. AerialWaste (Torres and Fraternali, 2023) is the principal labelled resource — on the order of 10⁴ tiles with 22 material categories — but it is RGB-only, and its site coordinates are withheld for legal reasons, preventing direct fusion with multispectral satellite imagery. CascadeDumpNet (2024) reports 84.6% mAP on Pléiades 0.5 m imagery, and vendor pipelines (Disaitek/Airbus on Pléiades Neo) claim ~95% operational performance, but neither releases a peer-reviewed, material-labelled benchmark. Global dumpsite datasets (Sun et al., 2023) prioritise localisation over material content. Cross-band generalisation studies further caution that models do not transfer cleanly across sensors with different band sets: GeoCrossBench (2025) reports 2-4× error increases (5-25% accuracy drops) under band shift, and foundation-model approaches such as DOFA (Xiong et al., 2024) attempt to address this through wavelength-conditioned encoders. The structural gap is therefore explicit: **there exists no public dataset combining VHR imagery, terrestrial waste, and material-level labels**, and no peer-reviewed material benchmark on Pléiades Neo. This thesis works within that gap by drawing labels from two complementary, imperfect sources — the coordinate-withheld AerialWaste material categories and the Lombardy asbestos-roof WFS ground truth — and by treating careful resizing and normalisation as a non-trivial methodological control (Corley et al., 2024), a point the cross-band literature shows is easy to underestimate.

## 5. Adapting Architectures and Generalizing Across Conditions

### 5.1 Adapting Pretrained Backbones to N Bands

The dominant transfer-learning paradigm in remote sensing inherits backbones pretrained on three-channel RGB imagery, which creates an immediate mismatch when the target sensor offers more spectral bands. Two families of adaptation strategies recur in the literature. The first modifies the input stem so that the additional bands can be ingested without discarding the pretrained representation. *Weight inflation* replicates or averages the RGB-trained first-layer filters across the extra channels, preserving the statistics learned on large natural-image corpora while allowing gradients to specialise the new channels during fine-tuning. *Random initialisation of extra bands* keeps the RGB stem intact and learns the supplementary channels from scratch, trading a slower start for an unbiased treatment of bands with no natural-image analogue. The second family, *late fusion*, processes spectral subsets through separate streams and merges their features at a deeper stage, decoupling the RGB prior from the multiband signal at the cost of additional parameters. These strategies are pragmatic rather than principled: none of them encodes the physical wavelength of a band, so a model adapted to one sensor's band layout does not transparently transfer to another's. This brittleness motivates the foundation-model line of work discussed below, and it is the central engineering concern when moving from the RGB-only baseline of Gibellini (2025) — the Swin-T/RSP backbone reaching F1 92.02% on AerialWaste with a 5.1% cross-region drop — toward a multiband classifier on WorldView-3 and Pléiades Neo.

### 5.2 Spectral Foundation Models

A recent thread attempts to remove the fixed-channel assumption entirely. DOFA (Xiong et al., 2024) conditions its patch-embedding weights on the physical wavelength of each input band through a hypernetwork, so a single model can in principle accept arbitrary band combinations from heterogeneous sensors. Prithvi extends masked-autoencoder pretraining to instruments that include SWIR, SpectralGPT targets the full spectral cube of multispectral imagery, and AnySat pursues modality-agnostic pretraining across resolutions and sensors. The appeal for this thesis is direct: a wavelength-conditioned model is the natural substrate for a band-ablation study, because the same architecture can be queried with RGB, with VNIR, and with VNIR+SWIR without changing the input stem by hand.

The honest assessment, however, is that foundation models do not yet reliably outperform well-tuned supervised baselines on specific downstream tasks. Benchmarks repeatedly show that a strong task-specific backbone, trained with careful augmentation and normalisation, matches or exceeds general-purpose geospatial models once the target domain is narrow and labels are available. Two confounders deserve emphasis. First, the GSD gap: most geospatial foundation models are pretrained predominantly on Sentinel-2-class imagery (10-60 m), whereas the operational target here is sub-2-metre VHR (WorldView-3 VNIR at 1.24 m, Pléiades Neo VNIR at 1.2 m); representations learned at decametric scale do not automatically capture the fine texture that discriminates waste deposits at VHR, and the resolution mismatch can erode any pretraining advantage. Second, evaluation hygiene matters: Corley et al. (2024) show that seemingly minor choices in resizing and input normalisation materially change measured performance, which means apparent foundation-model gains are sometimes artefacts of the comparison protocol rather than of the representation. The position taken here is therefore cautious — foundation models are evaluated as one option among several, not assumed superior.

### 5.3 Pansharpening and Native-Resolution Material Analysis

WorldView-3 delivers a 0.31 m panchromatic band alongside its 1.24 m VNIR and 3.7 m SWIR bands; Pléiades Neo similarly pairs a 0.3 m pan with 1.2 m VNIR. Pansharpening fuses the high-resolution pan with the coarser multispectral bands to produce a visually sharp, spatially fine multiband product. For detection and delineation this is attractive, but for *material discrimination* it is a liability: pansharpening injects spatial detail by redistributing intensity, and in doing so distorts the per-pixel spectral signatures that material classification depends upon. The methodological commitment adopted here, consistent with spectral-analysis practice, is to **classify on native-resolution multispectral data** and to reserve pansharpened products for localisation and visualisation. This is especially important for the SWIR bands, where the diagnostic absorptions live and where any spectral corruption would defeat the purpose of acquiring SWIR at all.

### 5.4 Generalization: Cross-Region, Cross-Sensor, Cross-Time

A model that scores well in-distribution but collapses on new geographies, sensors, or acquisition dates is operationally useless for an environmental agency. Gibellini (2025) already quantifies a 5.1% cross-region drop on RGB-only AerialWaste, and the survey of Fraternali et al. (2024) identifies RGB-only data as a key limitation precisely because colour alone is an unstable cue across illumination and landscape. The cross-band and cross-sensor case is sharper still: GeoCrossBench (2025) reports that models transferred across band configurations suffer 2-4× degradation, with absolute drops of 5-25%, exposing how fragile the fixed-channel adaptation strategies are when the sensor changes. A genuinely open and underexplored question — and one this thesis is positioned to probe — is whether adding spectral bands *narrows* the generalization gap (because chemistry-based signatures are more illumination- and geography-invariant than texture) or *widens* it (because each new sensor introduces its own band-layout and calibration idiosyncrasies that an adapted backbone overfits). The cross-time axis adds seasonal and weathering variation, relevant for asbestos-cement roofs whose spectra evolve as the matrix degrades (Cilia et al., 2015; Martínez-Sánchez et al., 2024). Treating generalization as a measured axis rather than an afterthought is therefore a deliberate design choice.

## 6. Gaps and Positioning

Synthesising the preceding sections, four gaps emerge. First, the operational waste-detection literature is overwhelmingly **RGB-only**: AerialWaste (Torres and Fraternali, 2023) annotates 22 material categories but on RGB imagery with coordinates withheld, CascadeDumpNet (2024) reaches 84.6% mAP on Pléiades RGB, and vendor systems such as Disaitek/Airbus report ~95% on Pléiades Neo without spectral discrimination. The added value of multiband data for *material* discrimination in waste detection is essentially unmeasured. Second, where multiband evidence exists, it is **adjacent rather than direct**: Aguilar et al. (2021) show VNIR→VNIR+SWIR ablation on WorldView-3 (OA 90.85→96.79→97.38) with a plastic-film/NDPI analogy, and the asbestos line (Saba et al., 2026, Macro-F1 97.6% on WV-3 VNIR; Bonifazi et al., 2026; Shepherd et al., 2025 on EnMAP, out of VHR scope) demonstrates material identification but not within an illegal-waste, risk-oriented pipeline. Third, the foundation-model and adaptation literature does not resolve whether spectral pretraining helps at VHR, leaving the **GSD-and-band-transfer question open**. Fourth, no existing work couples material identification to an **actionable hazard/risk framing** for environmental enforcement.

This thesis is positioned against these gaps with four hedged contributions. (1) A **two-dimensional evaluation matrix** crossing a band-ablation axis (R0 RGB → R1 +RedEdge/NIR → R2 full VNIR → R3 +SWIR) with a generalization axis (cross-region, cross-sensor, cross-time), so that the added value of multiband data is reported jointly with its robustness rather than as a single in-distribution number. (2) A **chemistry-versus-texture decomposition**, separating gains attributable to diagnostic spectral absorptions — chrysotile Mg-OH near 2.30-2.33 µm, plastic C-H at 1.215/1.730 µm, carbonate near 2.34 µm (Kokaly et al., 2017) — from gains attributable merely to higher-dimensional texture; the WV-3 SWIR-8 layout, which crowds asbestos, concrete, and plastic features into a single ~2.33 µm band, is acknowledged as a real bottleneck that bounds what SWIR can deliver. (3) A mapping from identified **material → EWC/List-of-Waste hazard class** (e.g. asbestos 17 06 05*) → **risk tier**, operationalising risk as hazard × exposure × magnitude (Fazzo et al., 2020) and aligning outputs with ARPA prioritisation instruments (PRAL, Indice di Degrado). (4) An **asbestos pilot demonstrator** on Lombardia, serving as a controlled, ground-truthed instance where the material signal is spectrally well-characterised, before generalising the framework to heterogeneous illegal-waste materials. All four contributions are framed as hypotheses to be tested empirically; the expectation that SWIR helps material discrimination is treated as a claim to be measured against the SWIR-8 bottleneck and the generalization axis, not as a foregone conclusion.
