# Spectral Band Selection Rationale

## Wavelengths Critical for Waste Material Discrimination

### Construction & Demolition Materials (Krauz et al., 2025)

Krauz et al. (2025) investigated the minimum number of spectral bands required to classify nine categories of construction and demolition waste (CDW)---aerated-autoclaved concrete, standard concrete, mortar, clay bricks, ceramic roof tiles, glazed ceramic tiles, extruded polystyrene (EPS), wood, and asphalt---using a SPECIM PFD4K-65-V10E hyperspectral camera spanning 400--1000 nm at approximately 3 nm spectral resolution across 768 contiguous bands. A multilayer perceptron classifier trained on reflectance features extracted from 20 x 20 pixel subsets demonstrated that conventional RGB channels alone yield roughly 77% overall accuracy, with the greatest confusion occurring among cementitious materials (concrete, mortar, and tile substrates) whose reflectance curves overlap substantially in the visible range due to shared cite composition (Krauz et al., 2025).

The central finding is that **two additional narrowband filters beyond RGB are sufficient** to raise classification accuracy to approximately 95%. Systematic evaluation of 127 possible combinations drawn from seven candidate wavelengths (tested at 25 nm increments from 425 to 975 nm, with FWHM bandwidths of 5, 20, 35, and 50 nm) identified two optimal spectral regions: a first filter in the **red/red-edge region near 650--750 nm** (capturing differential absorption between organic and mineral materials) and a second filter in the **near-infrared at 850--1000 nm** (exploiting fundamentally different NIR reflectance between organic materials such as wood and mineral substrates such as concrete). The single best configuration placed filters at central wavelengths of **775 nm and 975 nm with 20 nm FWHM**, achieving 94.8% overall accuracy. An alternative top-performing pair at **500 nm and 800 nm** was also identified, where the 500 nm channel captures green-light reflectance variation while the 800 nm channel provides NIR discrimination. Accuracy exhibited negligible sensitivity to bandwidth within the 5--50 nm FWHM range tested, indicating that even moderately broad satellite bands can capture the relevant spectral contrast (Krauz et al., 2025).

Per-material analysis revealed that concrete remained the most challenging class (87% accuracy) owing to persistent confusion with mortar and the reverse side of cladding tiles---all of which share cementitious binders. The NIR filter substantially reduced misclassification between brick and wood (from ~11% to near-zero), since organic cellulose and mineral clay exhibit divergent absorption behaviour beyond 800 nm. EPS (expanded polystyrene) was readily separable at all wavelength combinations due to its characteristically high, flat reflectance. The study was explicitly constrained to the VNIR range (400--1000 nm) and did not evaluate SWIR wavelengths beyond 1000 nm, leaving open the question of whether C--H and O--H absorption features in the 1400--2500 nm SWIR region could further improve discrimination among mineral classes (Krauz et al., 2025).

### Plastic / Macroplastics (Uhrin et al., 2025)

Uhrin et al. (2025) addressed the complementary problem of detecting and mapping terrestrial macroplastic waste (25--1000 mm items) using the eight SWIR bands of the WorldView-3 (WV3) satellite, combined with 1024-band laboratory reflectance spectroscopy of field-collected samples. The study area comprised a complex urban watershed along the US--Mexico border near San Diego, where field surveys identified several large unmanaged waste aggregations (80--150 m^2) in stream channels (Uhrin et al., 2025).

Laboratory spectroscopy of five common polymer types---high-density polyethylene (HDPE), low-density polyethylene (LDPE), polyethylene terephthalate (PET), polystyrene (PS), and polyvinyl chloride (PVC)---revealed **consistent SWIR absorption features near 1210, 1415, 1660, and 1728 nm**, with smaller consecutive absorptions between 2132 and 2313 nm. Per-polymer detail showed: PET absorbs at 1130, 1415, 1660, 1908, and 2132 nm; HDPE at 1210, 1430, and 1728 nm; LDPE at 1206--1213, 1400--1430, 1728, and 2308--2313 nm; PS at 1141, 1150, 1450, 1678, and 2167 nm; and PVC at 1215, 1417, 1537, 1716, 1732, and 2313 nm (Uhrin et al., 2025). The absorption features in the 1570--1730 nm region were present across **all five polymers** and overlapped with WV3 SWIR bands 2 through 4 (centered near 1572, 1661, and 1731 nm), making these bands the most broadly discriminative. The longer-wave features near 2100--2300 nm, captured by WV3 SWIR bands 5--8, provided additional discrimination for PET, PVC, and LDPE.

When the 1024-band laboratory spectra were convolved to the 8-band WV3 SWIR spectral response function, the diagnostic absorption features were largely preserved, and the image-derived polymer endmember correlated closely with the convolved laboratory spectra (r = 0.95, p < 0.01; Jeffries-Matusita and Transformed Divergence separability scores of 2.0, the theoretical maximum). A matched filtering classifier operating on the 8-band SWIR image achieved a **mapping precision of 92.5%** (185 true positives out of 200 validated polygons), rising to **95.3%** when restricted to high- and very-high-certainty detections (161/169 polygons). The 15 false positives included trees adjacent to structures, trucks, bare dirt lots, and geolocation errors of 1--2 pixels. Omission errors were not quantified due to the absence of simultaneous field validation at the 2020 image acquisition date (Uhrin et al., 2025).

Critically, the study found that WV3's **~3.7 m ground sampling distance** was sufficient to resolve urban aggregations of polymer-based debris but not individual macroplastic items. The minimum mapped cluster comprised approximately 20 contiguous pixels (~320 m^2), while field-documented aggregations ranged from 80 to 150 m^2. The authors explicitly dismissed Sentinel-2 and Landsat as unsuitable for plastic mapping: "sensors like Landsat and Sentinel-2 have limited applicability for mapping anthropogenic waste, since many urban targets are smaller than a decameter-scale pixel, and most plastic absorption features are not resolved by two broad SWIR bands" (Uhrin et al., 2025). This assessment reflects both the spatial limitation (20 m pixels cannot resolve sub-100 m^2 waste piles) and the spectral limitation (S2's two SWIR bands at ~1610 nm and ~2190 nm, each with >88 nm bandwidth, cannot capture the narrow polymer absorptions near 1210, 1415, and 1728 nm that require the finer spectral sampling of WV3's eight SWIR channels).

## Implications for Pipeline Design

The evidence from these two studies yields clear guidance for spectral band selection in the AerialWaste multispectral ablation pipeline. For **C&D waste discrimination**, the critical spectral information lies in the VNIR range: the red-edge (650--750 nm) and NIR (850--1000 nm) channels provide nearly all the discriminatory power needed to separate mineral from organic materials, with overall accuracy plateauing at approximately 95% using just five input features (RGB + two narrowband filters). Sentinel-2's red-edge bands (B5, B6, B7) and NIR bands (B8, B8a) cover these wavelengths adequately, though the 20 m pixel size limits detection to waste stockpiles exceeding roughly 400 m^2. A multispectral ablation study should therefore test S2 configurations that progressively add red-edge and NIR bands to a baseline RGB model, expecting the largest accuracy gains from the first one or two additions and diminishing returns thereafter, mirroring the pattern observed by Krauz et al. (2025).

For **plastic waste**, the situation is fundamentally different. The diagnostic polymer absorption features at 1210, 1415, 1660, and 1728 nm require narrow SWIR bands that Sentinel-2 does not provide: its B11 (~1614 nm, 91 nm FWHM) and B12 (~2190 nm, 175 nm FWHM) are too broad and too few to resolve the four critical absorption minima, and its 20 m spatial resolution cannot isolate waste aggregations below 400 m^2. WorldView-3, with eight SWIR bands at ~50--90 nm bandwidth and 3.7 m GSD, represents the current minimum viable spaceborne platform for this task, achieving 92--95% mapping precision for plastic aggregations above ~320 m^2 (Uhrin et al., 2025). In the ablation pipeline, plastic-specific band combinations should therefore be evaluated exclusively on WV3 or equivalent high-resolution SWIR imagery, with bands SWIR-2 through SWIR-4 (1572--1731 nm) as the primary feature set and SWIR-5 through SWIR-8 (2164--2329 nm) as secondary channels. When only Sentinel-2 data are available, the pipeline should not attempt polymer-specific classification and should instead rely on proxy indicators---such as anomalous albedo, texture, or contextual features---to flag potential waste sites for higher-resolution follow-up.
